To the right of the Town Hall, before your gaze drifts toward the Royal Theatre, the Grand-Place of Mons preserves a small alignment of façades that deserve more than a passing glance. These houses may seem modest beside the great municipal silhouette, but in their own way they recount several centuries of Mons history: the prestige of the old Burgundian Netherlands, the elegance of Renaissance architecture, the strength of civic Gothic, and the everyday life of a square that was as much a centre of power as a place of trade, meeting, and sociability.

The first remarkable façade is that of the Maison de la Toison d'Or, immediately to the right of the Town Hall. It catches the eye with its elegance of brick and blue stone, in an early seventeenth-century Renaissance style. In Mons, blue stone is never a mere material: it gives rhythm to façades, highlights openings, frames details, and recalls the city's roots in a territory of builders and stonecutters. Here it dialogues with brick in a balanced, sober composition, yet one charged with prestige.

The name of the house is no accident. The Golden Fleece refers to one of the most prestigious orders of chivalry in Europe, founded in the fifteenth century by Philip the Good, Duke of Burgundy and Count of Hainaut. Through this name and the coats of arms surrounded by the collar of the Golden Fleece, an entire chapter of Mons history reappears: that of a city integrated into the old Burgundian Netherlands, then marked by Spanish heritage. The façade is therefore not merely beautiful; it inscribes the Grand-Place within a far broader political and symbolic history, that of alliances, princely power, and urban prestige.

This detail matters, because Mons has never been an isolated city. Its history was written at the crossroads of influences: Hainaut, Burgundian, Spanish, French, Austrian, Belgian. On this façade, the coats of arms and the reference to the Golden Fleece recall an era when signs of power were also displayed on private houses. The Grand-Place was not only a backdrop: it was a stage, and each building played its part.

Today, the Maison de la Toison d'Or is also associated with the Salon Baronne Betty Duesberg, devoted in particular to Mons goldsmithing. This link is highly telling: it unites the nobility of symbols, the refinement of objects, and local craftsmanship. The house then becomes a bridge between architectural heritage and the decorative arts. It tells at once the ancient power of emblems and the finesse of work done by hand. In a city like Mons, where history is often read in the details, this façade is an excellent example of discreet yet dense heritage.

Right next door, the building known as the Maison Saint-Christophe, or the former Café du Commerce, offers a different atmosphere. Its darker, more severe façade contrasts with the Renaissance elegance of the Golden Fleece. Here the eye enters a register that is older, more vertical, more Gothic. The building is generally described as a sixteenth-century civic Gothic house. Its three stepped levels, stone string courses, corbelled arcatures, basket-handle doorway, and certain window details still allow one to imagine the appearance of an old urban dwelling, built to last but also to assert a presence in the commercial heart of the city.

This type of façade is precious, because it reminds us that Gothic does not belong only to churches. There is also a civic Gothic, that of merchants' houses, urban residences, places of exchange and trade. In Mons, this Maison Saint-Christophe bears witness to that architecture of the city, less spectacular than a cathedral, but just as revealing of everyday life and ancient social organisation.

The history of the building adds further interest. The house was notably known as the Café du Commerce, a name that immediately evokes the activity of the Grand-Place: conversations, appointments, business, circulating news, regulars, travellers, merchants, and Montois passing through. Before modern terraces and today's cafés, the square already lived to the rhythm of these places of sociability. People came to drink, talk, negotiate, comment on city life, or simply watch the movement of the square.

One anecdote makes this atmosphere especially vivid: at the end of the nineteenth century, before 1892, a room next to the Maison de la Toison d'Or is said even to have served, on busy days, as an annex to the drinking establishment of the Café du Commerce in the Maison Saint-Christophe. One can easily picture the scene: on market days, feast days, or days of great animation, the café overflows, conversations continue, doors open from one house to the other, and the Grand-Place becomes a vast popular open-air salon.

The Maison Saint-Christophe later knew other uses. It notably served as a police station, then housed the Tourist Office. This succession of functions is interesting: commerce, urban control, welcoming visitors. Through a single building, one crosses several ways of inhabiting the city. The house served the Montois in their daily lives, then helped present Mons to those who came to discover it. It has been listed as a monument since 10 November 1955, official recognition of its heritage value.

Observing these two façades side by side, one better understands the richness of the Grand-Place. The Town Hall naturally draws attention, but one must not forget the houses that surround it. They give the square its depth, its texture, its rhythm. The Maison de la Toison d'Or evokes prestige, coats of arms, Burgundian heritage, and the Renaissance. The Maison Saint-Christophe recalls civic Gothic, commerce, cafés, the police, tourism, and the ordinary life of a city in motion.

These buildings are not merely old façades. They are witnesses. They show that the Grand-Place of Mons is not a frozen décor, but a living space, transformed over the centuries. Every stone, every frame, every ancient name carries a trace: that of the powers that marked the city, the trades that enriched it, the inhabitants who animated it, and the visitors who continue to walk through it today.

Before continuing toward the Royal Theatre, take the time to look up. Between the Town Hall and the theatre, Mons slips a few pages of its history into a very short space. It takes only a few metres to pass from Burgundian chivalry to popular cafés, from the Renaissance to civic Gothic, from the prestige of coats of arms to the lively conversations of a trading Grand-Place. That is precisely what makes Mons charming: a city where great monuments impress, but where small façades often tell the most savoury stories.
